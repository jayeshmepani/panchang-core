# Verifying the Integrity of a Hindu Festival Calendar: An Analysis of Completeness, Computational Validity, and Swaminarayan Representation

This report presents a comprehensive deep research analysis aimed at verifying the completeness and accuracy of a provided list of Hindu (Sanatana) festivals, with a specific focus on the Swaminarayan sect. The investigation assesses the dataset against authoritative traditional texts, including the Puranas and Dharmashastra, to validate its content and computational logic. The scope of this research encompasses three core dimensions: first, verifying the accuracy of festival names, associated deities, descriptions, and regional attributions; second, assessing the validity of the embedded calculation rules based on classical calendrical systems; and third, identifying any significant festivals absent from the list, thereby ensuring a high degree of completeness. The analysis places special emphasis on distinguishing and accurately representing the unique festivals and theological nuances of the Swaminarayan tradition within the broader framework of Sanatana Dharma.

## Accuracy and Completeness of Pan-Indian Festivals

An examination of the provided festival dataset reveals a foundational understanding of the Hindu lunisolar calendar system, but also uncovers significant gaps in both the comprehensiveness of pan-Indian festivals and the precision of some descriptive elements. While many major observances celebrated across India are present, several prominent festivals are conspicuously absent, and other entries require refinement to ensure doctrinal and geographical accuracy. The dataset's reliance on tithi-based calculations is largely consistent with classical Indian astronomy [[10](https://www.scribd.com/document/819568239/Tithi-Calculations), [32](http://vedicastrology.wikidot.com/hindu-calendar)], yet the representation of certain events, especially those with strong regional identities, lacks the necessary granularity.

A primary area for improvement lies in the inclusion of festivals that are celebrated with immense fervor across diverse geographies. Notably absent are Janmashtami, the celebration of Lord Krishna's birth, and Dussehra (also known as Vijayadashami), which commemorates the victory of Lord Rama over Ravana and the triumph of good over evil [[51](https://www.scribd.com/document/510128058/HINDU-FESTIVALS-Bhaarateeya-Thyohaar)]. The omission of Janmashtami is particularly striking given its widespread popularity and the presence of related Krishna-centric observances in the list. Similarly, while Diwali is partially represented under the entry "Kali Puja / Diwali," this conflates the festival with a specific regional practice, primarily observed in Bengal, and fails to capture its multifaceted nature as the Festival of Lights. Diwali is widely celebrated for multiple reasons, including the return of Lord Rama to Ayodhya, the worship of Goddess Lakshmi for prosperity, and the appearance of Lord Dhanvantari [[51](https://www.scribd.com/document/510128058/HINDU-FESTIVALS-Bhaarateeya-Thyohaar)]. Creating a distinct entry for Diwali, calculated as the Trayodashi of the Krishna Paksha in the month of Kartika, would significantly enhance the dataset's completeness and reflect its pan-Indian character [[9](https://www.scribd.com/document/441887384/Hindu-Scripture-Book-2)].

The dataset contains an entry for "Krishna Janmashtami," but its calculation appears to be an error. It is listed with a `type` of `tithi`, `paksha` of 'Shukla', `tithi` of 8, and `month_amanta`/`month_purnimanta` of 'Shravana'/'Bhadrapada'. This date does not align with the widely accepted observance of Janmashtami, which falls on the Ashtami Tithi of the Krishna Paksha in the month of Shravana/Bhadrapada [[28](https://ro.scribd.com/document/434145273/List-of-Hindu-Festivals-Wikipedia)]. Correcting this calculation is paramount for the dataset's credibility. Another festival already present is Skanda Shashti, dedicated to Lord Murugan, which is a major event in South India [[28](https://ro.scribd.com/document/434145273/List-of-Hindu-Festivals-Wikipedia), [52](https://bcpat02.wixsite.com/heve/single-post/2016/03/13/13-skanda-shastimurgan-puran)]. While it exists in the list, its description could be expanded to better reflect its dual significance as both a Murugan festival and a form of Sun God worship, as noted in some traditions [[51](https://www.scribd.com/document/510128058/HINDU-FESTIVALS-Bhaarateeya-Thyohaar)].

The accuracy of festival descriptions can also be improved for greater clarity and doctrinal fidelity. For instance, the festival "Kanya Sankranti" is described simply as "Sun enters Kanya" with a note of "Worship of divine architect." While the latter part is correct, naming the deity Vishwakarma explicitly would add valuable detail [[5](https://www.scribd.com/document/232856737/Swaminarayan-Wikipedia-The-Free-Encyclopedia)]. Similarly, the description for Raksha Bandhan states "Brother-sister bond," which is accurate but omits its connection to the birth of Hayagriva, another important aspect mentioned in some traditions [[9](https://www.scribd.com/document/441887384/Hindu-Scripture-Book-2)]. Enhancing these descriptions will provide users with a more complete and nuanced understanding of each festival's significance. The festival "Sheetala Satam / Sheetala Saptami" is correctly attributed to Gujarat and associated with health-related prayers, but specifying that the fast involves offering cold foods to the goddess would further clarify the ritual practice [[13](https://www.scribd.com/document/423236503/Sarvopari-Bhagwan-Shri-Swaminarayan)].

Furthermore, the dataset's treatment of regional attributions requires careful scrutiny. The solar sankranti festivals are broadly labeled "Pan-India," which is an oversimplification. The cultural and religious importance of these events varies dramatically by region. Mesha Sankranti, for example, marks the solar new year but is celebrated with distinct local customs in different parts of India, such as Baisakhi in Punjab and Puthandu in Tamil Nadu [[9](https://www.scribd.com/document/441887384/Hindu-Scripture-Book-2)]. Its description should acknowledge this diversity rather than implying a monolithic national observance. Makara Sankranti is another case in point; while it is a harvest festival in the south known as Pongal, it also serves as the site for the massive Makar Mela pilgrimage at the Triveni Sangam in Prayagraj, a distinctly northern tradition [[9](https://www.scribd.com/document/441887384/Hindu-Scripture-Book-2)]. A more refined dataset might attribute these festivals to specific regions or highlight their varied significances to reflect the rich tapestry of Hindu culture.

In summary, while the existing data on pan-Indian festivals demonstrates a solid grasp of the lunar calendar, its completeness is compromised by the absence of major events like Janmashtami and Dussehra. The accuracy of some festival descriptions can be enhanced by adding specific details about deities and rituals, and the broad "Pan-India" attribution for solar sankrantis must be replaced with a more nuanced view that reflects India's profound regional diversity. Correcting calculation errors, such as the one for Janmashtami, is essential for maintaining the dataset's integrity.

## Computational Rules and Calendrical Validity

The computational framework underpinning the provided festival dataset is fundamentally sound, reflecting a robust understanding of the traditional Hindu lunisolar calendar system. The primary mechanisms employed—Tithi-based calculations and fixed-date markers—are rooted in ancient astronomical and astrological texts, providing a reliable method for determining the timing of religious observances. However, a deeper analysis reveals areas where the implementation can be refined for greater consistency, precision, and alignment with classical sources like the *Surya Siddhanta* and various Panchangams [[8](https://www.scribd.com/document/254363039/Hindu-Calendar), [32](http://vedicastrology.wikidot.com/hindu-calendar)].

The use of `tithi` as the core unit for festival determination is correct. A tithi is defined as the time it takes for the longitudinal angle between the Moon and the Sun to increase by 12 degrees, resulting in 30 tithis per lunar month [[11](https://www.scribd.com/document/867012008/The-Panchang-in-Vedic-Astrology)]. The dataset correctly utilizes parameters such as `paksha` (the fortnight, either Shukla/ waxing or Krishna/ waning), `month_amanta`, and `month_purnimanta` to specify the exact tithi within a particular lunar cycle. This methodology aligns with classical texts that describe the complex interplay between solar days, lunar days, and synodic months [[66](https://www.scribd.com/document/283178486/Essence-of-Dharma-Sindhu)]. The `resolver => 'classical'` designation indicates an adherence to this traditional, mathematically precise system rather than a modern interpolated one. The consistent application of these rules for recurring festival series, such as the four different Navaratris (Chaitra, Sharad/Ashvina, Ashadha, Magha) and the monthly Ekadashi fasts, demonstrates a clear and logical approach to calendrical computation [[62](https://www.scribd.com/document/352506519/Essence-of-Hindu-Festivals-and-Austerities)].

The dataset also shows sophistication in its handling of karmakala, or the specific time window during a tithi when an observance is valid. Entries include flags like `karmakala_type` set to values such as `sunrise`, `madhyahna` (midday), `nishitha` (midnight), and `pradosha` (twilight), along with `strict_karmakala` and `vriddhi_preference` settings [[11](https://www.scribd.com/document/867012008/The-Panchang-in-Vedic-Astrology)]. This level of detail is crucial because the start and end times of tithis vary daily, and many rituals must be performed only within a specific temporal boundary of the designated day. For example, the strict requirement for Krishna Janmashtami to be observed at midnight (`nishitha`) and the specific timings for Ganesh Chaturthi at midday (`madhyahna`) are correctly captured, reflecting established devotional practices [[28](https://ro.scribd.com/document/434145273/List-of-Hindu-Festivals-Wikipedia)]. The `vriddhi_preference` setting for festivals like Rama Navami and Maha Shivaratri, preferring the last possible tithi, is also a well-established rule in many Dharmashastra texts to maximize the spiritual benefit of the observance [[6](https://www.scribd.com/document/970336163/Assignment-Dsc-Edited)].

Despite these strengths, the dataset exhibits some inconsistencies and ambiguities that affect its overall validity. One significant issue is the inconsistent use of `amanta` versus `purnimanta` month designations. The Amanta system, prevalent in regions like Gujarat, considers the new moon day (Amavasya) as the beginning of the next month, whereas the Purnimanta system, common in eastern India, considers the full moon day (Purnima) as the start. Several entries, such as Sheetala Ashtami and Papmochani Ekadashi, correctly use the mixed `Phalguna amanta` / `Chaitra purnimanta` designation, reflecting this duality [[50](https://www.scribd.com/doc/289491390/Hindu-Vratas-and-Utsavas)]. However, for festivals like Chaitra Navaratri, the month is listed as 'Chaitra' for both systems, which can create ambiguity about the year's starting point for different ritual cycles. Standardizing the notation—for instance, consistently using the Amanta system where applicable—would improve clarity and reduce potential confusion.

Another area requiring attention is the generalization of the solar sankranti festivals. While the principle of the sun's transit into a zodiac sign is astronomically accurate, listing them all with a generic "Pan-India" tag and no specific regional context oversimplifies their calendrical role. In a more precise system, these events are often tied to the beginning of a solar month (e.g., Mesha Sankranti begins Mesha Maas). Integrating this relationship would make the solar component of the calendar more coherent with the lunar structure. Furthermore, while the `solar_sankranti` type is correctly defined, the descriptions lack the depth found in tithi-based entries. They do not mention the specific deities associated with each rashi or the unique regional celebrations that mark these transitions, limiting their utility beyond a basic astronomical marker.

Finally, the dataset includes a `fixed_date` type for "Vasi Uttarayan," which is listed as occurring on the 15th of the second month. This is a valid concept, as some festivals are indeed tied to a specific day of the Gregorian calendar. However, the description notes it is "Second day of the kite festival in Gujarat," which suggests it is part of a multi-day event. The festival itself, Uttarayan, is a significant winter solstice celebration in Gujarat, and its inclusion is relevant. However, its classification as a `fixed_date` might be less flexible than a `tithi`-based equivalent and may not always fall on the same day relative to the lunar calendar. Clarifying the exact rule for this festival and ensuring its description is precise would strengthen this entry.

In conclusion, the computational engine driving the festival list is largely accurate and sophisticated, correctly employing classical tithi-based calculations and advanced concepts like karmakala and vriddhi preference. To elevate its quality, the dataset should strive for greater consistency in its month nomenclature, refine the descriptions of solar sankranti festivals to be more informative, and ensure all entries, especially those with `fixed_date` types, have precise and unambiguous definitions.

## Specificity and Representation of the Swaminarayan Tradition

The provided dataset makes a deliberate and commendable effort to incorporate festivals specific to the Swaminarayan Sampradaya, a Vaishnava denomination founded in the early 19th century by guru Sahajanand Swami [[2](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Shailaja-Menon-3/publication/329123220_The_Swaminarayan_Sect_Contested_Reforms/links/63160b641ddd4470213aaa37/The-Swaminarayan-Sect-Contested-Reforms.pdf), [15](https://www.scribd.com/document/35401730/Cornell-Thesis-Rudert)]. The Swaminarayan sect has grown significantly, with organizations like the Bochasanwasi Akshar Purushottam Swaminarayan Sanstha (BAPS) playing a key role in its global expansion [[4](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2158244015623996), [31](https://www.scribd.com/document/857718282/PMFIAS-Prelims-Magnum-2025-09-History-Art-Culture-Society)]. The dataset successfully identifies several key figures within this lineage, including Bhagwan Swaminarayan himself, his spiritual successor Gunatitanand Swami, and contemporary leaders like Pramukh Swami Maharaj and Mahant Swami Maharaj [[36](https://www.scribd.com/document/845632732/Bhagatji-Maharaj-English), [54](https://www.scribd.com/document/711555518/KK-Adhiveshan-Satsang-FAQ-s-Booklet-2023-Revised-24-10-23)]. These festivals are correctly placed within the traditional Hindu lunar calendar, demonstrating an awareness of the sectarian calendar system. For instance, the Jayantis of Swaminarayan, Gunatitanand Swami, and Pramukh Swami Maharaj are all defined by specific tithi, paksha, and month combinations, which is the correct approach for tracking these annual events [[13](https://www.scribd.com/document/423236503/Sarvopari-Bhagwan-Shri-Swaminarayan), [21](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/328168726_An_Introduction_to_Swaminarayan_Hinduism)].

However, a critical analysis reveals a significant flaw in the dataset's structure that undermines the distinct identity of the Swaminarayan tradition. The most pervasive issue is the practice of merging Swaminarayan-specific observances with their non-sectarian counterparts, often separated by a forward slash (e.g., `'Rama Navami / Swaminarayan Jayanti'`). This conflation is problematic for several reasons. Theologically, it blurs the lines between celebrating an avatar of Vishnu (Lord Rama) and celebrating Bhagwan Swaminarayan, who is considered by followers to be the supreme, ultimate Godhead (Purushottam) [[5](https://www.scribd.com/document/232856737/Swaminarayan-Wikipedia-The-Free-Encyclopedia)]. While both are revered forms of God, they represent different levels of the divine hierarchy within the Swaminarayan faith's philosophy. By combining their birthdays, the unique theological significance of each day is obscured. Practically, it may lead to confusion among devotees about which figure they are honoring on a given day. A follower observing "Rama Navami / Swaminarayan Jayanti" might mistakenly perform Rama-centric rituals instead of the specific Swaminarayan puja prescribed for that day.

This structural issue extends to other festivals as well. For example, Sharad Purnima is presented as `/Sharad Purnima / Manekthari Punam / Gunatitanand Swami Jayanti`. While all three occur on the same day, they are distinct observances. Sharad Purnima is a pan-Hindu festival associated with Lord Krishna, Manekthari Punam is a specific Gujarati name for the festival, and Gunatitanand Swami Jayanti commemorates a pivotal leader in the Swaminarayan history [[13](https://www.scribd.com/document/423236503/Sarvopari-Bhagwan-Shri-Swaminarayan)]. Combining them into a single, long key prevents the creation of focused, accurate descriptions for each. A superior approach would be to create separate, distinct entries for each festival, allowing each to have its own unique description, deity attribution, and set of associated attributes. This would not only honor the distinct identity of the Swaminarayan tradition but also provide a more organized and searchable dataset.

Furthermore, the scriptural authority for these festivals is not fully leveraged. The Swaminarayan Sampradaya is built upon a foundation of sacred texts, namely the *Vachanamrut*, *Swamini Vato*, and the *Shikshapatri* [[54](https://www.scribd.com/document/711555518/KK-Adhiveshan-Satsang-FAQ-s-Booklet-2023-Revised-24-10-23)]. The dataset correctly identifies festivals like Shikshapatri Jayanti and Vachanamrut Jayanti, which commemorate the presentation and compilation of these scriptures [[1](https://www.scribd.com/document/333263141/Edit-KS), [42](https://www.scribd.com/document/750473205/Pragna-1-Paper-1-Q-A)]. However, the descriptions could be enriched by referencing the specific teachings or principles outlined in these texts that are celebrated on those days. For instance, Shikshapatri Jayanti is a day to reaffirm the moral and ethical code laid out in the *Shikshapatri*, which is considered the key to attaining salvation [[18](https://www.scribd.com/document/430783387/Shikshapatri-Sanskrit-Gujarati-English-eBook)]. Explicitly stating this connection would add a layer of authenticity and deepen the user's understanding of the festival's purpose.

The representation of other Swaminarayan-associated figures is also present but could be more complete. Jalaram Jayanti is included, celebrating the saint Jalaram Bapa, who is highly revered within the community [[28](https://ro.scribd.com/document/434145273/List-of-Hindu-Festivals-Wikipedia)]. Similarly, Bhagatji Maharaj Jayanti is noted [[36](https://www.scribd.com/document/845632732/Bhagatji-Maharaj-English)]. However, the festival of Goga Panchami, specifically the version observed in Gujarat which honors Goga Ji, is correctly identified but its link to the broader Swaminarayan community could be clarified, as Goga Maharaj is sometimes venerated alongside Swaminarayan. The festival of Holi is also included, but its specific association with the Swaminarayan tradition, which celebrates the divine pastimes of Krishna, could be highlighted more prominently.

In summary, while the dataset correctly identifies numerous festivals central to the Swaminarayan tradition, its current structure of merging these with non-sectarian holidays is a major weakness. To truly meet the research goal of validating the Swaminarayan representation, the dataset must be restructured to give each festival its own distinct identity. Separating merged keys, enriching descriptions with scriptural context, and clarifying the theological distinctions between avatars and the supreme being are essential steps for creating an accurate and respectful representation of this important branch of Hinduism.

## Identification of Missing Festivals and Regional Variations

Beyond the issues of accuracy and Swaminarayan representation, a thorough validation of the dataset reveals the absence of numerous significant festivals that are integral to the cultural and religious landscape of India. The provided list, while extensive, is heavily weighted towards observances popular in North and Western India, leaving vast regions and their unique traditions unrepresented. Identifying these omissions is crucial for achieving a truly comprehensive and inclusive festival calendar.

One of the most significant gaps is the lack of major festivals from South India. While some tithi-based festivals are universal, the Dravidian states celebrate events with deep local roots. For example, Pongal, a major harvest festival in Tamil Nadu, is not present. Though it occurs around Makara Sankranti, it has its own distinct rituals, songs, and significance that are not captured by the generic solar sankranti entry. Similarly, Thai Pongal, a multi-day festival dedicated to the Sun God Surya, is a cornerstone of Tamil culture and its absence is notable [[9](https://www.scribd.com/document/441887384/Hindu-Scripture-Book-2)]. Other important southern festivals like Ugadi (celebrated in Andhra Pradesh and Telangana) and Gudi Padwa (in Maharashtra) are included in the list, but their descriptions could be enriched to reflect their regional specificity [[9](https://www.scribd.com/document/441887384/Hindu-Scripture-Book-2)]. The festival of Skanda Sashti, dedicated to Lord Murugan, is celebrated with great enthusiasm in Tamil Nadu and Kerala, often involving elaborate processions and fasting [[28](https://ro.scribd.com/document/434145273/List-of-Hindu-Festivals-Wikipedia), [52](https://bcpat02.wixsite.com/heve/single-post/2016/03/13/13-skanda-shastimurgan-puran)]. While it is partially covered, a more detailed entry focusing on its South Indian context would be beneficial.

The Northeastern states of India possess a rich tapestry of indigenous and Hindu-influenced festivals that are entirely absent from the dataset. These communities have unique traditions that predate or exist parallel to mainstream Hinduism, showcasing a symbiotic relationship between agriculture, seasons, and spirituality [[44](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/401424027_Culture_and_Agriculture_in_India_Traditions_Indigenous_knowledge_and_Future_Pathways)]. The absence of festivals like Bihu in Assam, which marks the Assamese New Year and the agricultural cycle, leaves a major cultural void. This highlights the need for the dataset to move beyond a purely textual, Dharmashastra-based model and incorporate knowledge of these vital regional and tribal observances to achieve true completeness [[25](https://www.mdpi.com/2077-1444/14/1/85), [65](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/362067993_Indian_Folklores_Cultural_Narratives_-_Ethnopoetics_Tradition_and_Perspective)].

Furthermore, even within the regions covered by the dataset, several important festivals are missing. Karva Chauth, a fast observed by married women in North India for the longevity of their husbands, is present but its description could be more precise about the ritual of fasting until the sighting of the moon [[51](https://www.scribd.com/document/510128058/HINDU-FESTIVALS-Bhaarateeya-Thyohaar)]. The festival of Yogi Maharaj Jayanti, celebrating the birth of Brahmaswarup Yogi Maharaj, is listed, but this is a relatively minor festival compared to others in the Swaminarayan calendar [[36](https://www.scribd.com/document/845632732/Bhagatji-Maharaj-English)]. More importantly, the festival of Tulsi Vivah, the ceremonial wedding of the Tulsi plant to Lord Vishnu, is included but could be better contextualized as a significant event that marks the symbolic beginning of the wedding season in many Hindu households [[51](https://www.scribd.com/document/510128058/HINDU-FESTIVALS-Bhaarateeya-Thyohaar)]. The festival of Vasanta Panchami is also present, but its celebration as Saraswati Puja, dedicated to the goddess of knowledge, is a pan-Indian event of immense importance that could be highlighted more clearly.

## Synthesis of Findings and Recommendations for Dataset Refinement

The comprehensive analysis of the provided Hindu festival dataset confirms its value as a structured attempt to catalog religious observances based on traditional calendrical principles. The foundational logic, particularly the use of tithi-based calculations, is fundamentally sound and aligns with classical Hindu astronomy [[10](https://www.scribd.com/document/819568239/Tithi-Calculations), [32](http://vedicastrology.wikidot.com/hindu-calendar)]. The dataset successfully integrates a wide array of pan-Indian festivals and makes a concerted effort to include key observances of the Swaminarayan Sampradaya. However, to meet the high standards of accuracy, completeness, and specificity required by the research goal, several critical refinements are necessary. The primary challenges lie in correcting factual inaccuracies, expanding the dataset to include significant missing festivals, and restructuring the representation of the Swaminarayan tradition to preserve its unique identity.

The most urgent recommendations involve correcting errors and filling major gaps. First, the calculation for Krishna Janmashtami must be corrected to the standard date of Krishna Paksha Ashtami in the month of Shravana/Bhadrapada [[28](https://ro.scribd.com/document/434145273/List-of-Hindu-Festivals-Wikipedia)]. Second, the dataset is missing several cornerstone pan-Indian festivals, most notably Janmashtami, Dussehra/Vijayadashami, and a properly contextualized Diwali. Adding these entries with their correct calculation rules is essential for completeness. Third, the merging of Swaminarayan festivals with their non-sectarian counterparts, such as "Rama Navami / Swaminarayan Jayanti," must be rectified. This practice obscures the distinct theological identity of the Swaminarayan faith. The recommended action is to split these combined keys into separate, distinct entries for each festival. This will allow for unique, accurate descriptions and prevent doctrinal conflation.

To enhance the overall quality and usability of the dataset, several other improvements are advised. Descriptions for all festivals should be reviewed and enriched for clarity and depth. For example, specifying deities where appropriate (e.g., Vishwakarma for Kanya Sankranti) and detailing key rituals (e.g., offering cold food for Sheetala Satam) will provide richer context. The inconsistent use of `amanta` and `purnimanta` month designations should be standardized to improve the logical coherence of the calendar system. Finally, the regional attributions for festivals, especially the solar sankrantis currently labeled "Pan-India," should be revised to reflect the diverse ways these events are celebrated across different parts of the country, acknowledging their varied cultural and religious significances [[9](https://www.scribd.com/document/441887384/Hindu-Scripture-Book-2), [44](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/401424027_Culture_and_Agriculture_in_India_Traditions_Indigenous_knowledge_and_Future_Pathways)].

By implementing these targeted changes, the dataset can be transformed from a good initial draft into a definitive, authoritative, and comprehensive reference. The proposed refinements will not only verify the accuracy and completeness of the information but also elevate the representation of the Swaminarayan tradition, ensuring it is portrayed with the distinctness and respect it deserves within the broader tapestry of Sanatana Dharma. This process will result in a resource that is far more reliable for scholarly, educational, and devotional purposes.
