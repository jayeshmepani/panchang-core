**Yes, all four seasonal Navratris involve dedicated worship of specific forms (Devis) of the Goddess (Shakti/Durga) for each of the nine Ratris (nights/days), but it is not uniform across all four.** The two "Prakat" (manifest/public) Navratris follow the standard **Navadurga** (nine forms of Durga) sequence with one dedicated Devi per day. The two "Gupt" (secret/hidden) Navratris have more esoteric/tantric focus, often emphasizing the **Dasha Mahavidya** (ten wisdom goddesses) instead of (or in addition to) the Navadurga, though some devotional practices still reference the nine Durga forms.

### The Four Seasonal Navratris (Confirmed Across Hindu Texts & Traditions)
According to Shakta/Vaishnava Puranas and calendars like Drikpanchang, there are theoretically four Navratris, aligned with seasons/astronomical transitions (equinoxes/solstices). Only two are widely public; the other two are more subdued/esoteric:

- **Chaitra Navratri** (Vasanta/Spring, March–April): Prakat Navratri. Marks Hindu New Year start in many regions.
- **Sharad Navratri** (Autumn, September–October): Most popular (Maha Navratri). Public celebrations with Durga Puja, Garba, etc.
- **Ashadha Gupt Navratri** (Monsoon, June–July): Gupt (secret) Navratri. Also called Gupta or Gayatri Navratri in some texts.
- **Magha Gupt Navratri** (Winter, January–February): Gupt Navratri. Sometimes linked to Pausha/Magha month.

These align with the four yugas in some texts (e.g., Sharad is prominent in Kali Yuga).

### Dedicated Devis per Ratri: Day-Wise Breakdown
Each Navratri spans Pratipada (Day 1) to Navami (Day 9), with Dashami as victory/parana. Worship is of Shakti in specific forms.

#### 1. Chaitra Navratri & Sharad Navratri (Prakat Navratris)
**Yes — fully dedicated Navadurga sequence.** Each Ratri has one specific form of Durga (Navadurga), worshipped publicly with fasting, colors, bhog, and rituals. This is consistent across temples, calendars, and texts. The nine forms represent a spiritual progression (from strength to siddhi).

- **Day 1 (Pratipada)**: Shailaputri (strength, daughter of mountains).
- **Day 2 (Dwitiya)**: Brahmacharini (penance, knowledge).
- **Day 3 (Tritiya)**: Chandraghanta (courage, protection).
- **Day 4 (Chaturthi)**: Kushmanda (creation, energy).
- **Day 5 (Panchami)**: Skandamata (motherhood, wisdom).
- **Day 6 (Shashthi)**: Katyayani (victory, justice).
- **Day 7 (Saptami)**: Kalaratri (destruction of evil, fierce form).
- **Day 8 (Ashtami)**: Mahagauri (purity, peace).
- **Day 9 (Navami)**: Siddhidatri (perfection, siddhis).

This is standard for both Chaitra and Sharad. Chaitra often ends with Ram Navami; Sharad with Dussehra/Vijayadashami.

#### 2. Ashadha Gupt Navratri & Magha Gupt Navratri (Gupt Navratris)
**Partially yes, but with key differences — not strictly the same Navadurga sequence in tantric traditions.** These are for sadhaks/tantrics (often secret, under guru guidance, not public pomp). Focus is more on inner Shakti awakening, siddhis, and esoteric sadhana.

- **Primary worship in tantric/shakta sources**: **Dasha Mahavidya** (10 Wisdom Goddesses of Adi Shakti) — Kali, Tara, Tripura Sundari, Bhuvaneshwari, Bhairavi, Chhinnamasta, Dhumavati, Bagalamukhi, Matangi, Kamala. These represent cosmic forces (not a sequential "one per ratri" like Navadurga) and are invoked for bhoga + moksha. Some days also include Varahi (Saptamatrika form) for protection/prosperity. Gupt Navratris are explicitly distinguished: "Just as the nine forms... during Chaitra and Sharad..., during Gupt Navratri the worship of the Dus Mahavidya..."

- **In some devotional/panchang sources**: They still reference the **nine forms of Shakti/Durga** (Navadurga) day-wise, similar to Prakat ones (e.g., Day 1 Shailaputri, etc.). Drikpanchang describes Ashadha/Magha Gupta as "dedicated to the nine forms of Shakti." Magha-specific guides list full Navadurga day-wise pujas with bhog.

**Key distinction**: Gupt = hidden/tantric (mantras, yantras, secrecy; no public processions). Even when Navadurga is mentioned, the deeper practice layers Mahavidya sadhana. Not every source agrees 100% on day-wise mapping for Gupt, unlike the clear standardization for Chaitra/Sharad.

### Summary Table for Clarity
| Navratri Type       | Season       | Dedicated Devis per Ratri                  | Nature of Worship          |
|---------------------|--------------|--------------------------------------------|----------------------------|
| Chaitra            | Spring      | Standard Navadurga (9 forms, fixed sequence) | Public/devotional         |
| Sharad             | Autumn      | Standard Navadurga (9 forms, fixed sequence) | Public/devotional (most celebrated) |
| Ashadha Gupt       | Monsoon     | Primarily Dasha Mahavidya (10); some Navadurga | Esoteric/tantric, secret |
| Magha Gupt         | Winter      | Navadurga (in many guides) or Dasha Mahavidya | Esoteric/tantric, secret |

**Bottom line**: All four **do have** dedicated Devi worship for each Ratri (9 days of specific Shakti forms), but only the two Prakat Navratris universally follow the exact same Navadurga sequence as "dedicated Devi of each ratri." The Gupt ones emphasize tantric Mahavidyas more, making them different in practice and focus. This aligns with texts like Mahakala Samhita, where significance shifts by yuga, and Gupt are for advanced sadhana.
